Warm‑up: Simple SELECT
	1	List all employees.
	        SELECT * FROM Employees;

	2	Show only employee first name, last name, and salary.
	        SELECT Firstname, Lastname, Salary FROM Employees;

	3	List all students’ names and GPA ordered by GPA descending.
	        SELECT Firstname, Lastname, GPA FROM Students ORDER BY GPA DESC;

	4	List all products with price less than 40.
	        SELECT ProductID, Description, Price FROM Products WHERE Price < 40;

Filtering with WHERE
	5	Employees in California.
	        SELECT * FROM Employees WHERE State = 'California';

	6	Employees with salary greater than 80,000.
	        SELECT Firstname, Lastname, Salary FROM Employees WHERE Salary > 80000;

	7	Students from Arizona with GPA at least 3.5.
	        SELECT * FROM Students WHERE State = 'Arizona' AND GPA >= 3.5;

	8	Instructors who have training.
	        SELECT * FROM Instructors WHERE Training = TRUE;

	9	Products with price between 30 and 60 (inclusive).
	        SELECT * FROM Products WHERE Price BETWEEN 30 AND 60;

	10	Customers who live in Arizona.
	        SELECT * FROM Customers WHERE State = 'Arizona';

Sorting and Limiting
	11	List employees ordered by salary descending.
	        SELECT Firstname, Lastname, Salary FROM Employees ORDER BY Salary DESC;

	12	List the top 3 highest‑paid employees.
	        SELECT Firstname, Lastname, Salary FROM Employees ORDER BY Salary DESC LIMIT 3;

	13	List products ordered by price then description.
	        SELECT * FROM Products ORDER BY Price, Description;

Basic Aggregates
	14	Average salary of all employees.
	        SELECT AVG(Salary) AS AvgSalary FROM Employees;

	15	Average salary by region.
	        SELECT Region, AVG(Salary) AS AvgSalary FROM Employees GROUP BY Region;

	16	Count of students per state.
	        SELECT State, COUNT(*) AS NumStudents FROM Students GROUP BY State;

	17	Total sales amount in the Sales table.
	        SELECT SUM(Amount) AS TotalSales FROM Sales;

	18	Total quantity ordered per product.
	        SELECT ProductID, SUM(Quanity) AS TotalQuantity FROM Orders GROUP BY ProductID;

GROUP BY + HAVING
	19	Regions where average employee salary is over 75,000.
	        SELECT Region, AVG(Salary) AS AvgSalary FROM Employees GROUP BY Region HAVING AVG(Salary) > 75000;

	20	States with more than 1 student.
	        SELECT State, COUNT(*) AS NumStudents FROM Students GROUP BY State HAVING COUNT(*) > 1;

	21	Customers who have ordered more than 50 total items.
	        SELECT CustomerID, SUM(Quanity) AS TotalQty FROM Orders GROUP BY CustomerID HAVING SUM(Quanity) > 50;

Joins (Inner, Left)
	22	Orders with product descriptions (INNER JOIN).
	        SELECT o.OrderID, o.Quanity, p.Description, o.Price FROM Orders o INNER JOIN Products p ON o.ProductID = p.ProductID;

	23	Orders with customer names.
	        SELECT o.OrderID, c.Firstname, c.Lastname, o.Quanity, o.Price FROM Orders o INNER JOIN Customers c ON o.CustomerID = c.CustomerID;

	24	Sales with corresponding employee names (if EmployeeID matches).
	        SELECT s.SalesID, e.Firstname, e.Lastname, s.Region, s.Amount FROM Sales s INNER JOIN Employees e ON s.EmployeeID = e.EmployeeID;

	25	Students with instructor state (Students → Instructors by name).
	        SELECT st.Firstname, st.Lastname, st.GPA, i.State AS InstructorState FROM Students st INNER JOIN Instructors i ON st.Instructor = i.Name;

	26	LEFT JOIN to find customers who have never ordered anything.
	        SELECT c.CustomerID, c.Firstname, c.Lastname FROM Customers c LEFT JOIN Orders o ON c.CustomerID = o.CustomerID WHERE o.CustomerID IS NULL;

Subqueries
	27	Employees with salary above the overall average.
	        SELECT Firstname, Lastname, Salary FROM Employees WHERE Salary > (SELECT AVG(Salary) FROM Employees);

	28	Students whose GPA is above the average GPA of all students.
    	    SELECT Firstname, Lastname, GPA FROM Students WHERE GPA > (SELECT AVG(GPA) FROM Students);

	29	Customers who have placed at least one order for product 1.
	        SELECT * FROM Customers WHERE CustomerID IN (SELECT DISTINCT CustomerID FROM Orders WHERE ProductID = 1);

	30	Products that have never been ordered.
	        SELECT * FROM Products WHERE ProductID NOT IN (SELECT DISTINCT ProductID FROM Orders);

Updates, Inserts, Deletes (Practice on Backup Tables)

	31	Copy all employees into EmployeesBackup.
	        INSERT INTO EmployeesBackup SELECT * FROM Employees;

	32	Give everyone in EmployeesBackup a 5% raise.
	        UPDATE EmployeesBackup SET Salary = Salary * 1.05;

	33	Set bonus to 1000 for Marketing team in EmployeesBackup.
	        UPDATE EmployeesBackup SET Bonus = 1000 WHERE Team = 'Marketing';

	34	Copy all students into StudentsBackup.
	        INSERT INTO StudentsBackup SELECT * FROM Students;

	35	Mark StudentsBackup records with GPA below 3.0 as 'Probation'.
	        UPDATE StudentsBackup SET Status = 'Probation' WHERE GPA < 3.0;

	36	Delete all EmployeesBackup rows where Years < 2.
	        DELETE FROM EmployeesBackup WHERE Years < 2;


Five Challenges:
You need to figure out what query to write and provide the results.

Scenario 1 – Sales Region Staffing
The sales director wants to know if every region that appears in the Sales table has at least one employee assigned to that same region in the Employees table.
Challenge: Write a query that lists any regions that appear in Sales but do not appear in Employees.Region. If your result is empty, it means all sales regions are staffed.

Scenario 2 – Duplicate Customer Addresses
A marketing analyst suspects some duplicate households in the Customers table where different customers share the exact same address.
Challenge: Write a query that finds all street/city/state/zip combinations that belong to more than one customer, and show the address along with how many customers share it.

Scenario 3 – Instructor Coverage for States
The academic coordinator wants to check whether every state that has at least one student also has at least one instructor located in that state.
Challenge: Write a query that lists any states that appear in Students.State but do not appear in Instructors.State.

Scenario 4 – Cross-Selling Opportunity
The product manager believes that customers who buy higher-priced items (Price ≥ 50 in Orders) might be good candidates for a premium upsell.
Challenge: Write a query that returns each customer who has at least one order where Price >= 50, along with the count of such high-priced orders for that customer.


Scenario 5 – Underutilized Employees
HR wants to find employees who never appear in the Sales table, so they can investigate whether those employees are mis-assigned or underutilized.
Challenge: Write a query that lists all employees from Employees who do not have any matching row in Sales (based on EmployeeID).
